A Case Study in Rotation

Rotation in a Single Star World

Stars are born with range of rotation rates.

Low-mass stars slow down due to magnetic braking.

  • Rotation is a fundamental property of stars, and studying its evolution can tell us about stellar interiors, magnetic fields, and mass-loss.
  • Our understanding of rotation is summarized in this figure by Gallet & Bouvier (2015), which shows rotation distributions for clusters at different ages.
  • Young clusters show wide range of rotation rates, up to an order of magnitude
  • At old clusters, rotation rates slow down and converge due to magnetic braking
  • Convergence predicts a unique relation between rotation and age

Gyrochronology

Rotation can measure ages

  • Gyrochronology is an attempt to use rotation period and mass to derive age
  • This figure is from calibration of gyrochronology.
  • Dashed lines indicate gyrochronology relations at fixed age.
  • Pleiades shows high variability, as expected
  • Hyades shows matching gyro line
  • Old stars calibrated to the Sun, because rotation has been hard to measure.
  • Until... the Kepler satellite!

Kepler

The Kepler field is thought to be old

  • Kepler observed a single field for over 4 years looking for transiting planets
  • That field is pointed out of the disk of the Milky Way
  • Should be biased toward old ages
  • Found transiting planets through precise photometry
  • Also makes it a good candidate for starspot variability...

Kepler

Rotation measured through starspot variability

  • Kepler measured rotation periods through starspot modulations
  • Modulations occur as spots pass in front of the star
  • The largest such study was done by McQuillan

  • Many unexpected results from McQuillan's analysis

Nature of Rapid Rotators

Misclassified Subgiants

  • Subgiants can masquarade as rapid rotators as pointed out by van Saders & Pinsonneault (2012)
  • Rapidly rotating subgiants from above the Kraft break, where they don't have convective envelopes, can retain their rapid rotation as they expand and cool
  • Without a way to distinguish subgiants, they could be a contaminant.
  • Another is confusion...

Nature of Rapid Rotators

PSF Contaminants

  • Kepler pixels are 4 arcseconds on a side.
  • The source of variability may not be the target
  • This has been explored with planet detections and eclipsing binaries, not very much with rotation
  • Figure shows the Kepler pixels around an eclipsing binary. Inside each pixel is a light curve.
  • The orange shading is the aperture used for photometry.
  • The eclipse signal is not strongest at the center of the aperture, but off to the side
  • Very cool dwarfs have inefficient winds and remain rapidly rotating.
  • The option that we explored in the paper is tidally-synchronized binaries

Nature of Rapid Rotators

Tidally-synchronized Binaries

  • Tidally-synchronized binaries are binaries with orbital periods so short that the two components become tidally locked
  • Previous investigations have put that limit at around 10 days.
  • In the solar neighborhood, 4% of binaries have periods less than 10 days.
  • We explore whether the tidally-synchronized population can explain the rapid rotators.

Metallicity Impact

Metallicity has small impact

Show the empirical metallicity trend

  • Use APOGEE subsample to compare width of main sequence with and without metallicity
  • Right shows luminosity distribution after correcting for metallicity. Right shows distribution assuming they have the same metallicity
  • Both distributions are fit to a double-Gaussian
  • The width shifts slightly, from 0.9 mag to 1.1 mag
  • Given this finding, we draw a photometric binary threshold at 0.3 mag above the main sequence. Although the exact threshold doesn't substantially affect our results.
  • It should be noted that McQuillan excluded eclipsing binaries from their analysis. To understand the TSB population, they need to be included.
